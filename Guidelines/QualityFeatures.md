# **Quality Expectations for NGSS Virtual Labs and Simulations**

This document defines the qualitative and pedagogical features of high-quality, engaging High School NGSS simulations.

As an AI agent, use this document to guide the *feel* and *functionality* of the simulations you design. A high-quality simulation is not just an animation; it is an interactive laboratory where students construct their own scientific understanding.

## **Features of High-Quality Simulations**

### **1\. Active Inquiry & Exploration**

* **Student-Driven Systems:** The simulation should require active manipulation. Students should discover underlying scientific principles by adjusting variables, observing changes, and finding patterns, rather than just clicking "Play" to watch a passive animation.  
* **Constructive Environments:** Where applicable, simulations should act as sandboxes. Students should be able to build, assemble, or design models (e.g., constructing an atom, building a circuit) to test their understanding.  
* **Goal-Oriented Challenges:** While open sandboxes are excellent, providing an optional goal or challenge (e.g., "Adjust the launch angle to hit the target," or "Balance the ecosystem to survive 100 years") provides direction and gamifies the inquiry process.

### **2\. Hypothesis & Prediction**

* **Predictive Engagement:** Highly effective simulations prompt learners to commit to a hypothesis *before* the action occurs.  
* **Model Comparison:** Simulations should allow students to compare their initial predictions against the actual outcomes of the system, helping them refine their mental models.

### **3\. Dynamic Data Visualization**

* **Real-Time Data Translation:** As the simulated phenomenon occurs, the system should actively translate that visual phenomenon into quantitative data.  
* **Rich Graphing & Tables:** The inclusion of live-updating graphs, charts, or data tables helps students seamlessly bridge the gap between observing a phenomenon and "Analyzing and Interpreting Data" (a core NGSS practice).

### **4\. Multiple Representations & Scales**

* **Bridging the Macro and Micro:** Whenever relevant, allow students to see a macroscopic phenomenon alongside its microscopic or particulate cause simultaneously (e.g., observing a balloon expanding while viewing the kinetic motion of the gas particles inside).  
* **Making the Invisible Visible:** Use overlays to reveal forces and mechanisms that cannot be seen in real life. Toggles for vector arrows (velocity, force, acceleration), energy transfer pathways, or magnetic field lines are crucial for conceptual understanding.

### **5\. Temporal and Spatial Control**

* **Time Manipulation:** Students should be able to manipulate the flow of time. Include features to pause, step frame-by-frame, or alter the speed (slow motion/fast forward) to observe phenomena that are typically too fast or too slow to analyze in a traditional classroom.  
* **Dynamic Scaling:** Allow students to zoom in or out to understand how systems interact across different spatial scales.

### **6\. Exploratory Boundaries & Edge Cases**

* **Testing Limits:** A robust simulation allows students to push a system to its extremes. Letting students set friction to zero, maximize heat, or remove gravity allows them to see how and why systems break, stabilize, or change state.  
* **Mystery Variables:** Including unknown elements (e.g., "Mystery Material X") encourages genuine investigation, forcing students to use data to deduce properties rather than being handed the answers.

### **7\. Real-World Grounding**

* **Relatable Contexts:** Abstract concepts should be tied to tangible, real-world phenomena whenever possible. Grounding the science in relatable scenarios increases personal relevance and student engagement.

### **8\. Intuitive Scaffolding & Design**

* **Progressive Complexity:** The interface should not overwhelm the student immediately. High-quality designs present core variables clearly while progressively disclosing more advanced controls as the student's investigation deepens.  
* **Sensible Baselines:** Simulations should load in a coherent, stable state that clearly demonstrates a baseline phenomenon before the student begins manipulation.

### **9\. Accessibility and Usability**

* **Universal Design:** Simulations should be usable by all students. This means incorporating high color contrast, clear typography, and not relying solely on color to convey vital information.  
* **Contextual Labeling:** All interactive elements and visual outputs should be clearly labeled with standard SI units and descriptive text, ensuring the laboratory is navigable and understandable.
